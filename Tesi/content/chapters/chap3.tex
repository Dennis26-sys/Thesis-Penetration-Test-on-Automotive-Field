\chapter{Threat and Vulnerability Analysis}\label{ch:Threat_analysis}

\section{Automotive Threat Analysis}\label{sec:automotive_threat_analysis}

Modern vehicles are rapidly evolving into being Connected and Autonomous Vehicles (CAVs), which has dramatically increased their cyber-physical attack surface. New automotive architectures are not closed systems, but rather complex, distributed networks where safety-critical components interact and communicate with infotainment systems, external vehicle-to-everything (\gls{V2X}) interfaces, and cloud-based \gls{OEM} infrastructures.

From the cyber-security point of view this interconnection brings severe risks.
A successful intrusion no longer concerns data privacy but directly endangers human life by potentially overriding safety-critical cyber-physical functions (e.g., braking, steering, or powertrain management). For the development of resilient mitigation techniques like Dedicated Security Elements (DSE), a systematic analysis of automotive specific threats, vulnerabilities and potential attack vectors is necessary.
In this section it is provided a taxonomy of the main threats that modern automotive networks are exposed to, with a special focus on the vulnerabilities of \glspl{ECU}, internal communication buses, positioning systems and external telemetry.

% --- SUBSECTION: ECU ATTACKS ---
\subsection{Vulnerabilities and Attacks on ECUs}
Electronic Control Units (\glspl{ECU}) are embedded microcontrollers dedicated to the management of specific vehicular sub-systems, ranging from low-priority body comfort modules to high-priority Powertrain and \gls{ADAS}. While built for high reliability and real-time operation, \glspl{ECU} have certain structural weaknesses:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Firmware Tampering and Reverse Engineering:} Attackers attempt to reverse engineer the ECU firmware in an effort to reconstruct internal logic, extract proprietary algorithms (e.g. Seed-Key routines) or find zero-day vulnerabilities \cite{vandenherrewegen2018beneath}.
    This is typically done by obtaining memory dumps through physical debugging interfaces like JTAG or Nexus, or by exploiting unprotected bootloaders.
    \item \textbf{Malicious Reflashing and Ransomware:} If the cryptographic signature verification in the bootloader is not or poorly implemented, an attacker can use standard diagnostic sessions to overwrite the \gls{ECU} firmware. This allows the execution of arbitrary code, persistent backdoors or ransomware attacks that encrypt configuration files, resulting in a permanent Denial of Service (DoS) of the module\cite{Firmwareattack}.

    \item \textbf{Sensor Spoofing and Manipulation:} Control loops in \glspl{ECU} are executed using analogue and digital sensor inputs. By altering the physical environment like injecting false ultrasonic, radar or camera data, the \gls{ECU} is forced into making erroneous operational decisions, leading to unexpected vehicle behaviour \cite{petit2015remote,radar_spoofing2026}.
    
    \item \textbf{Over-The-Air (OTA) Interception:} Having wireless interfaces for remotely updating firmware allows for man-in-the-middle (MitM) attacks. If the \gls{OTA} orchestration channel does not have strong mutual authentication and end-to-end encryption, adversaries may be able to eavesdrop, tamper, or revert firmware payloads \cite{OTArollback}.
    
    \item \textbf{Node Isolation and Bus-Off Attacks:} These attacks exploit the inherent fault confinement mechanism specified by the \gls{CAN} standard. The health of each \gls{ECU} is monitored using 2 internal counters which are Transmit Error Counter (\gls{TEC}) and Receive Error Counter (\gls{REC}). With precise bit-flipping or target fuzzing synchronised at microsecond level, an attacker can deliberately force the \gls{TEC} of the target \gls{ECU} over the threshold of 255. This forces the victim \gls{ECU} into a permanent “Bus-Off” state, isolating it from the network entirely \cite{BusOff}.
A Bus-Off attack can be successfully performed with dedicated hardware for real-time bit manipulation, like Canari, SPIRE or microcontrollers customised on \gls{FPGA}.

\end{itemize}

Compromised \glspl{ECU} rarely constitute the final objective of an attacker. Instead, they serve as initial entry points for \textit{lateral movement}. An attacker could leverage a highly vulnerable infotainment \gls{ECU} on a low-priority Comfort \gls{CAN} bus, compromise it and use the Central Gateway \gls{ECU} to access the high-priority Powertrain \gls{CAN} bus to escalate privileges and take control of safety-critical modules.

% --- SUBSECTION: CAN BUS COMPROMISE ---
\subsection{Compromission and Core Vulnerabilities of the CAN Bus}
CAN bus is still the backbone of internal automotive communications due to its low cost, deterministic scheduling and robust error handling. However, the protocol was natively designed for closed environments and misses fundamental cybersecurity properties exploited by the following attack typologies:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Sniffing and Eavesdropping:} Because \gls{CAN} is a broadcast bus, each node in the bus receives every frame transmitted. Messages are sent in plaintext so any attacker with physical or logical access to the bus can intercept the traffic. This data can be used to reverse engineer the network matrix (\gls{DBC} files), monitor telemetry or analyse driver behaviour for external exploitation \cite{buscemi2023survey}.
    
    \item \textbf{Replay Attacks:}  In the absence of monotonic counters, timestamps or cryptographic freshness identifiers, an attacker can passively listen to valid \gls{CAN} frames and replay them at a later time. Examples include capturing door-unlock commands to bypass immobilizers, injecting high-speed telemetry when the vehicle is stationary or forcing an \gls{ECU} to accept a legacy, vulnerable firmware version (downgrade attack) \cite{miller2015remote}.
    
    \item \textbf{Fuzzing and Code Injection:} Attackers can inject a large volume of randomised or semi-structured CAN packets into the bus. This technique is used to find unhandled exceptions in the \gls{ECU}'s communication stack, which can cause unexpected software crashes, temporary Denial of Service, or unauthorised transitions between diagnostic states.
    
    \item \textbf{Data Integrity and Masquerading Faults:} The \gls{CAN} protocol does not authenticate the sender of a message. Any node can masquerade as any another by sending frames with a specific \gls{SID}. In multi-packet transmissions (such as \gls{UDS} ISO-TP segmentation), malicious payload can be injected mid-stream,  payload parameters can be modified, or frame structures can be corrupted to invalidate legitimate data buffers.
\end{itemize}

% --- SUBSECTION: DoIP SOME/IP ---
\subsection{IP attacks: DoIP and SOME/IP}

% --- CONTINUAZIONE DELLA SOTTOSEZIONE AUTOMOTIVE ETHERNET (DoIP e SOME/IP) ---
When analysing the automotive diagnostic ecosystem over IP, attacks targeting the \gls{DoIP} layer can be formalized into three critical threat categories:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Authentication Avoidance via Session Hijacking:} \gls{UDS} provides logical security boundaries like Service 0x27 (Security Access), but the underlying \gls{DoIP} layer does not support transport-level authentication natively and is thus completely dependent on application-layer components to provide this feature. As \gls{DoIP} supports multiple parallel \gls{TCP} sessions at the same time, \gls{OEM} configurations that are not implemented well can result in cross session vulnerability states. In particular, if a single valid diagnostic session completes the \gls{UDS} Security Access handshake successfully, the unlocked security state may be inadvertently applied globally to all concurrent unauthenticated \gls{TCP} sessions. This allows an external actor to execute limited diagnostic commands without the needed cryptographic authentication algorithm \cite{DoIPAttack}.
    
    \item \textbf{Man-in-the-Middle (MitM) via Vehicle Announcement Spoofing:} In the first phase of the edge discovery, vehicles periodically broadcast \gls{DoIP} \textit{Vehicle Announcement} messages to identify themselves to diagnostic testers. An attacker can inject fake announcement packets with a malicious IP address. If the test chooses the spoofed announcement, the communication channel is established directly with the attacker's infrastructure rather than the legitimate vehicle, allowing data interception and command injection \cite{DoIPAttack}.
    
    \item \textbf{Diagnostic Denial of Service (DoS):} This threat affects the availability of the diagnostic infrastructure. The attacker can desynchronise the state machine of the diagnostic tester by injecting false status parameters during the handshake or execution of the testing such as "Ready" status when in fact it is not or vice versa, which leads to unhandled timeouts and crash of active session.
\end{itemize}

Apart from \gls{DoIP},  the Scalable Service-Oriented MiddlewarE over IP (\gls{SOME/IP}) protocol is also popularly used for high-performance intra-vehicle communication. Since native \gls{SOME/IP} specification lacks built-in mechanisms for data integrity, payload encryption, and source origin authentication, it is structurally vulnerable to standard network exploitation vectors, including:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Insert Attacks:} The malicious injection of arbitrary \gls{SOME/IP} packets to invoke remote procedures or publish fake events.
    \item \textbf{Replay Attacks:} Interception of valid \gls{SOME/IP} message blocks for retransmission during a different operational cycle.
    \item \textbf{Eavesdropping:} Passive sniffing of plaintext middleware traffic over unencrypted Automotive Ethernet links, revealing internal system states.
    \item \textbf{Message Modification and Dropping:} Active manipulating real-time payloads or intentionally destroying packets (packet dropping) to break the data consistency of service-oriented architectures.
\end{itemize}

% --- SOTTOSEZIONE: GPS ---
\subsection{GNSS and GPS Manipulation: Jamming and Spoofing Mechanics}
The Global Positioning System (GPS), as part of the overall Global Navigation Satellite System (\gls{GNSS}), is the main source for spatial-temporal localisation in autonomous and connected vehicles. The coordinates (latitude, longitude, and altitude) calculation is based on the passive reception of radio signals from a plurality of satellites, by means of the mathematical principle of trilateration using accurate satellite ephemeris and atomic clock timestamps. \gls{GNSS} signals received at the Earth’s surface are very weak, and these systems are very sensitive to two types of interference:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{GPS Jamming:} The intentional emission of high power radio frequency noise on the designated \gls{GNSS} operational frequency bands. This leads to a low Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) that prevents the vehicle's \gls{GNSS} receiver from isolating and decoding the legitimate satellite signals, leading to a complete loss of positioning capability and navigation \gls{DoS}.
    
    \item \textbf{GPS Spoofing:} A sophisticated attack where a malicious transmitter broadcasts fake \gls{GNSS} signals at a slightly higher power than the real signals, thus taking over the receiver's tracking mechanisms. GPS spoofing can be classified into three levels of architectural complexity:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textit{Basic-Level Spoofing:} Defined as the generation of unsynchronised and randomized \gls{GNSS} signal patterns. Modern automotive receivers are adequately equipped to detect and isolate these as the injected telemetry structures violate fundamental protocol compliance standards.
        \item \textit{Intermediate-Level Spoofing:} \gls{GNSS} coordinate sets are compliant with the standard structure. While capable of deceiving standard receivers into reporting wrong spatial coordinates, they usually present sharp, discontinuous transitions (spatial jumps) in comparison to historical telemetry, thus making them detectable with a baseline plausibility assessment.
        \item \textit{Advanced-Level Spoofing (Meaconing):} The most advanced form, where the real \gls{GNSS} signals are recorded and rebroadcast with incrementally calculated mathematical propagation delays. This slow drift subtly leads the autonomous routing logic of the vehicle onto a wrong trajectory without triggering abrupt telemetry anomalies.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Vehicle-to-Everything (V2X) Cooperative Attack Surface}
Vehicle-to-Everything (\gls{V2X}) technologies, which include Vehicle-to-Vehicle (V2V) and Vehicle-to-Infrastructure (V2I) do not require the attacker to be physically close to the target. This turns cooperative driving interfaces that exchange real-time traffic, obstacle and intersection precedence information into distributed remote attack vectors.

While traditional network threats like Denial-of-Service or eavesdropping apply broadly to any wireless infrastructure, the V2X paradigm introduces a subset of novel, domain-specific attack vectors. Based on the comprehensive analysis provided in \cite{V2Xthreats}, these threats directly exploit cooperative logic and vehicular trust mechanisms:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Sybil Attacks:} A very destructive structural attack in which a single malicious node simultaneously creates and controls a large fleet of fictitious virtual identities.  The adversary can exploit cooperative consensus protocols, induce artificial traffic congestion, or maliciously force autonomous routing engines to yield right-of-way through coordinated injections of telemetry data from non-existent vehicles.
    \item \textbf{Impersonation:} The active forgery of a legitimate user to execute malicious actions. By masquerading as an emergency service vehicle or a trusted Roadside Unit (RSU), the attacker can inject safety-critical messages in a trusted environment.
    \item \textbf{Free-riding:} A selfish behaviour vector for network resource distribution. In this scenario, a compromised or misconfigured node constantly consumes incoming cooperative data and safety maps from neighbouring vehicles but actively suppresses its own telemetry transmissions. This information asymmetry deteriorates collective situational awareness and poisons localized collaborative driving models.
    \item \textbf{Location Tracking:} A passive surveillance vector that exploits the mandatory broadcast nature of V2X cooperative awareness messages. An attacker can use a distributed network of receivers to capture unencrypted continuous telemetry signatures such as velocity profiles and spatial coordinates, to reconstruct extensive historical routes of specific targets, violating basic user privacy.
    \item \textbf{Identity Revealing:} The targeted privacy violation vector is designed to disclose sensitive and identifiable information such as the owner's name, license plate, address or public-key certificates, to correlate a specific vehicle directly with its physical user \cite{V2Xthreats}. Adversaries can launch this attack with wireless eavesdroppers and by extracting long-term subscriber identifiers. Furthermore, real-world vulnerabilities in connected automotive services such as the Hyundai Blue Link mobile architecture demonstrate that attackers may exploit software vulnerabilities or malicious infotainment applications to compromise highly sensitive driver data such as emails, voice recordings, credentials, and PIN numbers \cite{cisa2017hyundai}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Regulations and methodologies}\label{sec:regulations}
Contemporary automotive cybersecurity has to evolve from ad-hoc security patches towards standardized risk management frameworks. Regulatory bodies and international standard organizations have defined mandatory compliance requirements and engineering methodologies to enforce systematic protection throughout the vehicle lifecycle.
\subsection{UNECE R155 regulation}
The United Nations Economic Commission for Europe (\gls{UNECE}) Regulation No. 155 is a binding regulatory framework that makes the vehicle type approval conditional to the implementation of a certified Cyber Security Management System (\gls{CSMS}) \cite{unece2021r155}. R155 is a global standard and applies to all major markets around the world. It puts the burden of securing vehicles on original equipment manufacturers (\glspl{OEM}) and mandates that they take security measures across the supply chain.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{images/R155.png}
    \caption[UNECE R155 Framework]{UNECE R155 Framework defining the core obligations for automotive type approval (Source: \cite{schaedlich2024complying}).}
    \label{fig:R155_regulation}
\end{figure}
Compliance with the \gls{UNECE} R155 framework requires action on four key organisational pillars \cite{schaedlich2024complying} as shown in Figure \ref{fig:R155_regulation}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Managing vehicle cyber risks:} \glspl{OEM} must develop comprehensive protocols for risk identification, validation and management across the entire vehicle ecosystem.
    \item \textbf{Securing vehicles by design:} Native security controls should be embedded in the automotive architecture through the value chain to minimise the overall attack surface.
    \item \textbf{Fleet detection and response:} Manufacturers need to monitor active vehicle fleets for ongoing threats and respond to security incidents in a timely manner.
    \item \textbf{Secure software updates:} Protect against the possibility that future changes such as Over-the-Air (\gls{OTA}) updates could compromise the safety of vehicles or the regulatory validation.
\end{itemize}

In this regulatory context, the use and deployment of automated penetration testing software has an operational, vital role. Such a tool directly addresses the validation requirements under the \textit{Managing vehicle cyber risks} and \textit{Fleet detection and response} pillars. With R155, \glspl{OEM} can no longer rely solely on theoretical risk assessments; they are legally required to validate and demonstrate the effectiveness of their security controls against realistic attack profiles prior to type approval, as well as throughout the vehicle's post-production lifecycle. In this work, the developed software automates the execution of targeted attack vectors on the diagnostic bus, translating abstract security requirements into empirical reproducible penetration testing routines.

In addition, \gls{UNECE} R155 sets challenging compliance targets but purposefully does not prescribe a specific and standardized engineering approach. Instead the regulation provides practical guidance in \textbf{Annex 5}, which contains an extensive taxonomy of reference cyber threats, vulnerabilities and targeted attack scenarios. This appendix acts as an informal checklist for automotive security teams, emphasising important testing surfaces like diagnostic interface manipulation, session hijacking, and malicious firmware flashing. Therefore, the penetration testing suite developed in this thesis is in full accordance with the spirit of \textbf{Annex 5}, by implementing specific attack models for the systematic evaluation of the vehicle's resilience against threat patterns.
\subsection{TARA methodology}
To put the risk management objectives set by UNECE R155 into practice, the ISO/SAE 21434 standard proposes the Threat Analysis and Risk Assessment (TARA) method. TARA provides an engineering approach for systematic identification of assets, vulnerability assessment and risks measurement during development phase.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.90\textwidth]{images/TARA.png}
    \caption[TARA steps]{The six core engineering steps of the TARA process according to ISO/SAE 21434 (Source: \cite{rad2024understanding}).}
    \label{fig:TARA_steps}
\end{figure}

The \gls{TARA} execution lifecycle is based on the cyclic architecture shown in Figure \ref{fig:TARA_steps} and consists of six interconnected phases \cite{rad2024understanding}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Item definition:} Defining the boundaries, components, and operating environment of the automotive system under consideration.
    \item \textbf{Asset identification:} Assessing the components to determine their value and mapping th specific cybersecurity properties (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability).
    \item \textbf{Impact rating:} Assessment of the operational, safety, financial, and privacy impacts of a compromised asset.
    \item \textbf{Threat analysis:} Identifying potential attack vectors and malicious actions targeting the defined system.
    \item \textbf{Risk determination:} Assessing the probability of successful attack exploitation and comparing it with the severity of the impact.
    \item \textbf{Cybersecurity goals:} Specify the top-level security requirements and mitigations that will reduce the calculated risk to tolerable levels.
\end{enumerate}

The conventional implementation of the \gls{TARA} approach is highly reliant on qualitative matrices and subjective expert judgements, especially for the assessment of the parameters of attack feasibility in Phase 5. Under standard ISO/SAE 21434 guidelines, attack feasibility is calculated based on factors such as required elapsed time, specialist expertise, window of opportunity and equipment complexity. However, without practical empirical data these metrics might be too conservative or inaccurate.

This methodological limitation is directly addresses by the penetration testing software engineered in this thesis, as it acts as an empirical validation layer of the \textit{Threat analysis} (Phase 4) and \textit{Risk determination} (Phase 5) steps. The tool translates theoretical threat scenarios to measurable and reproducible cybersecurity metrics by executing real-world automated attack vectors against targeted automotive assets such as the UDS diagnostic interface. If the automated suite can desynchronise a diagnostic state machine or bypasses a security seed-key handshake within milliseconds, it provides undeniable, quantitative evidence that the attack feasibility is high. Therefore, this penetration testing framework allows to base the TARA risk calculation on physical evidence, so that following \textit{Cybersecurity goals} (Phase 6) are correctly adapted to verified, architectural as opposed to hypothetical vulnerabilities.

\section{UDS Protocol Vulnerabilities}\label{sec:UDS_vulnerabilities}

Based on the diagnostic architecture formalised in Chapter \ref{ch:uds_protocol} and the STRIDE threat modelling framework defined in Section \ref{subsec:STRIDE}, this section systematically analyses the inherent security flaws of the ISO 14229 standard. The mechanics of the protocol such as session management and security access links are functional for operational workshops but their deployment in connected \gls{EE} ecosystems creates exploitable architectural vulnerabilities. 

Recently, the mapping of specific Service Identifiers (\glspl{SID}) to the STRIDE dimensions has been studied in security researches presented at the IEEE Conference on Communications and Network Security (CNS) \cite{UDS_vulnerability}, to demonstrate how these deterministic diagnostic interfaces can be weaponised as shown in Figure \ref{fig:SID_vulnerability}. 

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{images/UDS_vulnerability.png}
    \caption[UDS vulnerability analysis]{UDS vulnerability analysis mapped via STRIDE methodology (Source: \cite{UDS_vulnerability}).}
    \label{fig:SID_vulnerability}
\end{figure}

The structural weaknesses found in the core diagnostic sub-services expose the vehicle infrastructure to multiple high-impact attack surfaces, which are detailed in the following subsections.
\newpage
\subsection{Replay Vectors on Security Access (SID 0x27)}
The \textit{Security Access} routine is based on a challenge-response verification as seen in Section \ref{sec:security_access}. An architectural vulnerability occurs when the Pseudo-Random Number Generator (\gls{PRNG}) embedded in the \gls{ECU} firmware has low entropy. In such cases, the challenges generated are not random enough. An adversary that can sniff the bus could record many valid authentication exchanges and construct a deterministic lookup table composed of seeds and relative keys. If the \gls{PRNG} recycling is not adequate and a duplicated seed is re-issued, the attacker can perform a replay attack by sending the previously captured valid key, thus breaking the security layer without reverse-engineering the underlying cryptographic function.

\subsection{Exhaustive Brute-Force Exploitation (SID 0x27)}
In addition to cryptographic predictability, the practical deployment of SID 0x27 frequently suffers from inadequate key-length enforcement. When the security keys assigned to critical diagnostic privileges (such as flashing routines or execution overrides) utilize truncated bit-lengths, the absolute key space is constrained. If the target ECU firmware lacks stateful anti-brute-force controls—such as exponential timing delays or permanent session lockouts after consecutive authentication failures—an automated attacker can mount a high-speed, exhaustive search loop, querying key values sequentially until full session escalation is achieved.

\subsection{Direct Memory Access and Secret Extraction (SIDs 0x23 / 0x3D)}
The operational primitives \textit{Read Memory By Address} (0x23) and \textit{Write Memory By Address} (0x3D) allow low-level interactions with the volatile (RAM) and non-volatile (Flash) memory segments of the controller. If these services are left completely exposed—or accessed following a successful SID 0x27 bypass—they present a critical Information Disclosure risk. An attacker can command a full memory dump to extract raw compiled firmware binaries. Reverse-engineering of these binaries reveals proprietary logic, embedded cryptographic assets, master vehicle PIN configurations, and hardcoded security material.

\subsection{Resource Exhaustion and DoS Infiltration (SID 0x3E)}
While the \textit{Tester Present} (0x3E) command is natively used to keep a diagnostic session alive by inhibiting inactivity timeouts, its message cadence can be subverted to execute a local Denial of Service (DoS). By flooding a targeted ECU with high-frequency SID 0x3E frames (or repetitive diagnostic requests), an attacker can monopolize the shared network medium and saturate the microcontroller’s execution queue. This processing overhead blocks the application layer from running its deterministic control loop, effectively preventing safety-critical operational routines from executing in real time.

\subsection{Anti-Forensics via Diagnostic Log Deletion (SID 0x14)}
The \textit{Clear Diagnostic Information} (0x14) service provides a mechanism to purge non-volatile memory segments containing Diagnostic Trouble Codes (DTCs) and active freeze-frame telemetry. In a post-compromise scenario, this capability serves as a highly effective anti-forensic instrument aligned with the Repudiation threat profile. A malicious actor can execute a multi-stage network exploit and subsequently issue a global SID 0x14 request to wipe all system error logs. This operation erases the digital trail of the intrusion, blinding onboard Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS) and severely compromising subsequent forensic auditing.

\subsection{Arbitrary Data and Configuration Tampering (SID 0x2E)}
The \textit{Write Data By Identifier} (0x2E) service allows modifications to persistent configuration parameters, localized calibration offsets, and variant coding tables within the ECU. Unauthorized exploitation of this service yields severe Tampering risks with immediate cyber-physical consequences. By injecting fraudulent payloads via SID 0x2E, an attacker can alter the vehicle's dynamic handling behavior, falsify internal sensor metrics, or intentionally induce safe-state downshifting, directly undermining the functional safety parameters of the automotive platform.

\section{Attack Vectors on the Diagnostic Bus}
\label{sec:attack_vectors_diagnostic_bus}

While the preceding sections have provided a comprehensive analysis of UDS vulnerabilities and formalized the theoretical threat landscape using the STRIDE methodology, a crucial distinction must be made regarding the scope of this work. The primary objective of this thesis is not to map out complete, end-to-end malicious attack paths or to simulate complex infrastructure-wide compromises. Instead, this research focuses on a defensive and analytical paradigm, aiming to demonstrate that these specific, protocol-level vulnerabilities are inherently present within the ISO 14229 standard and are practically exploitable under realistic constraints.

Consequently, rather than focusing on complex weaponization chains, this study isolates the core structural flaws of the diagnostic layer to verify their susceptibility to targeted network infiltration. To bridge the gap between theoretical risk assessment and practical validation, the operational exploitability of these flaws will be explicitly demonstrated in the subsequent section. Through the implementation of dedicated, non-destructive validation scripts presented in Chapter \ref{ch:attacks}, where it will provided concrete evidence of how these deterministic diagnostic routines can be subverted, establishing a clear baseline for the development of necessary automotive intrusion detection and mitigation strategies.

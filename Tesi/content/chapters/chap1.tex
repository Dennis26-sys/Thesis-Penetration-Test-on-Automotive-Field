\section{Evolution of Automotive Architecture}\label{sec:automotive_evolution}

For several decades the design of vehicles was mainly focused on providing a reliable mechanical infrastructure that would be both secure and economical. However with the advancement of embedded technology the paradigm shifted toward providing software-driven platforms that provide services to the user to enhance the commodity of the transportation.

To support this digital transition, the underlying Electrical and Electronic (E/E) architecture had to evolve through three main topological models\cite{srmtech_vehicle_architecture_2025}:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Distributed architecture (The legacy model):} In early electronic systems, every new feature—such as anti-lock braking (ABS) or airbag deployment required a dedicated Electronic Control Unit (ECU). These units were in a closed network state and would not interact with external environments.
    \item \textbf{Domain control architecture (The current standard):} In this division the architecture is organised into domains according to functionality. Typically, these domains include Powertrain, Chassis, Body, ADAS, and Infotainment.
    \item \textbf{Zonal Architecture (The Next-Generation Paradigm):} The latest evolutionary step organises the vehicle's network based on physical location (e.g., front, rear, left, right) rather than logical function. Localised Zonal Control Unit collect data from nearby components and stream it to a central ECU, drastically reducing the amount of wiring necessary and allowing the use of strategic high speed wiring.
\end{enumerate}


\subsection{Functional Domains and Network Requirements}\label{subsec:network_requirements}
The current adopted model classifies ECUs based on the requirements for data throughput, timing determinism, and fault isolation \cite{Automotivecommunications}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Safety-Critical Domains (Powertrain and Chassis):} Units that manage core functionalities such as the Engine Control Module (ECM) or the ABS need low latency, typically ranging from 1 to 10 milliseconds, and absolute time determinism. Because a delayed message could result in catastrophic failure, these zones require strict \textit{fault isolation}. If an ECU fails, the communication protocol must alert the rest of the network.
    \item \textbf{High-Bandwidth Domains (ADAS and Telematics):} Advanced Driver Assistance Systems process continuous data streams from camera sensors and others real-time driver assistance systems. These systems require both high throughput and deterministic scheduling to perform safety-critical actions, such as automated braking, are executed without any delay.
    \item \textbf{Low-Criticality Domains (Body and Comfort):} This domain is responsible for non critical components such as climate control, seat adjustments and interior lighting. These networks operate on relaxed latency constraints and low bandwidth requirements, making them ideal for more economical protocols.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Structural Limitations and Protocol Evolution}
The presence of these different functional domains quickly revealed severe structural limitations within early E/E architectures, incentivising the development of high-speed in-vehicle communication protocols:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Moving from Traditional CAN to CAN-FD:} The traditional Controller Area Network (CAN) bus has been the basis of automotive networking for decades. However its maximum data rate of 1 Mbps and payload limit of 8 bytes per frame became a critical bottleneck. As the number of onboard ECUs increased, networks suffered from severe traffic saturation. This led to the introduction of CAN Flexible Data-rate (CAN-FD) \cite{canfd_comparison}, which mitigates these limits by increasing data throughput up to 5 Mbps and expanding the payload up to 64 bytes per frame.
    \item \textbf{The Transition to Automotive Ethernet:} Despite the bandwidth improvements of CAN-FD, modern data-heavy applications require transmission speeds far beyond the capabilities of legacy fieldbuses. This demand has led the adoption of Automotive Ethernet (standardized under IEEE 100BASE-T1 and 1000BASE-T1) \cite{siemens_automotive_ethernet}, which provides high-bandwidth, bidirectional communication (from 100 Mbps to over 1 Gbps) over a single unshielded twisted pair of copper wires.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Convergence and Automotive Cybersecurity Implications}
This transition from isolated, low-bandwidth buses to high-speed, interconnected topologies has drammatically changed the automotive cybersecurity landscape. Traditionally, low critical domains were physically separated from safety-critical networks. Nevertheless, producer requirements for over-the-air (OTA) firmware updates, remote cloud diagnostics, and sophisticated infotainment connectivity have forced these distinct domains to be linked via centralized gateways
\cite{srmtech_vehicle_architecture_2025}.

This architectural convergence leads to severe security vulnerabilities, which are now governed by the \textit{ISO/SAE 21434 Automotive Cybersecurity Engineering standard} \cite{iso21434_2021}. While traditional safety engineering ensures that a system is robust towards random hardware faults, cybersecurity engineering must ensure the containment of intentional, malicious exploits.

Because low-criticality domains like the Infotainment system possess external wireless interfaces (Bluetooth, Wi-Fi, Cellular V2X), they represent the primary point of entry for an attacker \cite{miller2015remote}. If the network lacks cryptographic boundary enforcement or strong gateway-level filtering, a malicious actor can inject unauthorized diagnostic payloads into a low-criticality bus. Attackers can then exploit internal routing mechanisms to perform a lateral movement and compromise safety-critical domains.

\subsection{Rationale for CAN Protocol Analysis}
Despite the rapid deployment of high-bandwidth solutions such as Automotive Ethernet, the CAN and CAN-FD protocols remain the de facto standards for real-time powertrain, chassis, and body control due to their low cost, robustness, and deterministic arbitration. Therefore, understanding the internal functionalities, intrinsic design limitations, and underlying vulnerabilities of the CAN protocol is essential for securing modern E/E architectures. The following section describes in detail its signalling mechanism, frame structure, and security weaknesses.

\section{CAN Communication Protocol}\label{sec:can_protocol}
The Controller Area Network (CAN) is a multi-master, asynchronous serial bus protocol designed by Robert Bosch GmbH in the 1980s to replace complex point-to-point vehicle wiring harnesses. It operates on a differential two-wire bus consisting of CAN High (CAN\_H) and CAN Low (CAN\_L) lines. Communication relies on two logical states defined by voltage differences: \textit{dominant} (logical 0) and \textit{recessive} (logical 1). Because a dominant state actively drives the bus voltage difference, it always overwrites a recessive state, enabling robust collision resolution without data corruption.

\subsection{CAN Data Frame Structure}
The CAN protocol transfers data across the network using specific frame types, the most notable being the standard Data Frame (defined in the CAN 2.0A specification). A standard CAN Data Frame is divided into distinct functional fields, each serving a specific role in synchronization, identification, data payload transmission, and error checking.

As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:can_frame_structure}, the standard Data Frame consists of the following sequential fields:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Start of Frame (SOF):} A single dominant bit (0) that synchronizes the clocks of all nodes on the bus after a period of idle network activity.
    \item \textbf{Arbitration Field:} Composed of an 11-bit Identifier (ID) and a single Remote Transmission Request (RTR) bit. The identifier defines the priority of the message; lower numerical ID values represent higher priority. The RTR bit distinguishes between a data frame (dominant 0) and a remote request frame (recessive 1).
    \item \textbf{Control Field:} A 6-bit field that includes the Identifier Extension (IDE) bit, a reserved bit (r0), and the 4-bit Data Length Code (DLC). The DLC specifies the exact number of bytes contained in the subsequent payload, ranging from 0 to 8 bytes.
    \item \textbf{Data Field:} The core payload of the message, containing from 0 to 8 bytes (0 to 64 bits) of application-specific parameters, such as sensor readings or control commands.
    \item \textbf{Cyclic Redundancy Check (CRC) Field:} A 16-bit field containing a 15-bit checksum derived from the preceding frame bits, followed by a single recessive CRC Delimiter bit. Nodes use this field to detect bit errors during transmission.
    \item \textbf{Acknowledgment (ACK) Field:} A 2-bit field consisting of the ACK Slot and an ACK Delimiter. The transmitting node sends a recessive bit during the ACK Slot; any receiving node that validates the frame integrity overwrites this slot with a dominant bit, confirming successful reception.
    \item \textbf{End of Frame (EOF):} A sequence of 7 consecutive recessive bits indicating the definitive termination of the Data Frame.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Security Properties and Architectural Vulnerabilities}
\label{subsec:can_security_properties}

When the CAN protocol was standardized, automotive networks were physically isolated, closed systems. Consequently, the protocol was designed to maximize dependability, real-time determinism, and hardware efficiency, completely omitting cryptographic security measures. Analyzing CAN through the lens of modern information security paradigms (the CIA triad) reveals critical structural deficiencies:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Lack of Authentication and Integrity (No Source Verification):} CAN frames lack any origin identifiers or cryptographic signatures. Messages are broadcast to the entire bus, and receiving nodes filter them based solely on the Message ID, which identifies the data type (e.g., engine speed) rather than the transmitter. As a result, any node connected to the bus can masquerade as another by injecting frames with identical IDs. The 15-bit CRC field only protects against random hardware bit-errors caused by electromagnetic interference, offering no protection against intentional malicious modifications.
    \item \textbf{Lack of Confidentiality (Open Broadcast Nature):} Because CAN uses a shared, broadcast medium, every node on the bus can read every transmitted frame. There is no native encryption layer at the data link or physical levels. An attacker who gains physical or logical access to the bus can perform passive eavesdropping, capture all network traffic, and reverse-engineer proprietary vehicle telemetry.
    \item \textbf{Vulnerability to Denial of Service (Arbitration Exploitation):} The deterministic priority mechanism, while beneficial for safety-critical real-time scheduling, introduces a major vulnerability. An attacker can continuously flood the bus with dominant bits or maximum-priority frames (ID \texttt{0x000}). Because of the non-destructive bitwise arbitration, lower-priority legitimate traffic is perpetually blocked, resulting in a total network Denial of Service (DoS).
    \item \textbf{Susceptibility to Cyber-Physical Attacks (Error Handling Exploitation):} The native fault-confinement mechanism of CAN can be weaponized. When a node detects too many transmission errors, it automatically transitions from an \textit{Error Active} state to an \textit{Error Passive} state, and eventually into a \textit{Bus-Off} state, which physically disconnects it from the network. Attackers can exploit this by precisely injecting dominant bits during the transmission of a target ECU, forcing it into a permanent Bus-Off state and effectively neutralizing a safety-critical component.
\end{itemize}
